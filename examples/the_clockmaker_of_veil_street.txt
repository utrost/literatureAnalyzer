CHAPTER I

Ada had swept the workshop floor every morning for three years, and every morning Silas Vane pretended not to notice. He sat at his bench beneath the tall window, his glass screwed into one eye, coaxing life back into the small dead watches the town brought him. Ada watched his hands more than she watched the broom. They were old hands, spotted and slow, but they never trembled over the works of a clock.

"You are staring again," Silas said, without looking up.

"I want to learn the tower," Ada said. It was the boldest thing she had ever said to him, and she said it to the floor.

The great clock of Veil Street stood at the end of the lane, four faces above the market, and it had kept the town's hours since before Ada was born. Silas wound it every seventh day. He had never once let her climb the stair with him.

"The tower is not for apprentices," he said. But that evening, when he thought she had gone home, Ada saw him take a small brass key from a drawer and turn it over in his fingers a long while, as though it weighed more than brass should weigh. She said nothing. She wanted the tower, and she had learned that wanting a thing in Veil Street meant waiting for it.

CHAPTER II

The winter that year came early and hard, and with it came the trouble. On the first cold morning the great clock stopped.

The town noticed the way a body notices a stilled heart. Ada was in the workshop when the bell that should have rung the eighth hour did not ring, and she looked up to find the old man already on his feet, his face grey. He took his coat and the brass key and went out into the frost without a word to her, and she followed him because she could not help it.

Bram was waiting in the square, as half the town was waiting, stamping their feet and blowing on their hands. Bram had apprenticed to the wheelwright and thought himself a man of the world for it. "Your master's clock is dead, Ada," he said, not unkindly. "They're saying he's too old to keep it."

"He is not too old," Ada said, though she had seen how the old man leaned on the railing of the tower stair, how long he stood before he trusted his foot to the first step. He climbed out of her sight. When he came down an hour later his hands, for the first time in all the years she had watched them, were shaking.

"It is not the cold," he told her quietly, so the crowd would not hear. "It is something in the escapement I cannot reach. My eyes are not what they were." He looked at her then as though deciding something. "Come to the workshop tonight. There is a thing you should see."

CHAPTER III

He gave her the notebook by candlelight, and her whole life turned over on its spine.

It was a small book bound in cracked leather, filled edge to edge with drawings of gears and escapements in a careful, slanting hand. Ada knew the hand. She had a single letter written in it, kept in a box beneath her bed.

"My father made this," she said.

"Your father made the clock," said Silas. "He built the tower works when he was younger than you are now. I was his apprentice, girl, the way you are mine. When the fever took him I promised him I would keep it turning, and I promised him I would not tell you until you were ready to carry it yourself." He closed her hands around the book. "I have kept one of those promises too long."

Ada could not speak. All her wanting of the tower, she saw now, had been a wanting she was born into and never understood. Bram, who had come to the door with bread from his mother and stood forgotten in the dark, heard every word, and had the grace to set the bread down and go.

"The escapement he drew here," Silas said, tapping a page. "He knew it would fail one day. He left the cure in his own hand. But I cannot see to do it, and you have never climbed the stair."

"Then I will climb it," Ada said.

CHAPTER IV

The council came the next morning, and they did not come kindly.

They stood in the square in their heavy coats, three of them, and they spoke of the electric bell they could buy from the city, a bell that needed no old man and no dead clockmaker's daughter, a bell that would ring on a wire and never stop. The old man stood before them with his back very straight and said that the clock could be mended, and they looked at his shaking hands and did not believe him.

"Let the girl try," said Bram, loudly, from the edge of the crowd, and the council laughed, and Ada felt the laugh go through her like cold water. She had never in her life been so afraid, and she had never been so certain of what she meant to do.

She climbed the tower stair alone. The old man could not manage it now and would not let her see him fail to. At the top the great works stood in the grey light, vast and patient and stopped, and for a long moment Ada stood among the still wheels of her father's making and felt that she would surely fail him, and Silas, and the whole waiting town below. She opened the notebook to the page he had marked. She set her father's drawing beside her father's clock. And she began, in the cold, to work.

CHAPTER V

The bell rang the noon hour across Veil Street, steady and warm, and the whole market went silent to hear it, and then the whole market cheered.

Ada came down the tower stair with grease to her elbows and her father's notebook against her chest, and the council said nothing at all, which from a council is a kind of surrender. Bram reached her first and lifted her clean off the last step, laughing, and she laughed too, though there were tears cutting clean lines through the dust on her face.

The old man was waiting at the workshop door. He did not embrace her; he was not a man who embraced. But he took the brass key from his pocket, the key he had turned over so many evenings, and he pressed it into her greased and aching hand.

"It is yours now," Silas said. "It always was."

Ada wound the great clock of Veil Street every seventh day after that, and taught the winding to Bram's children when they were old enough to want it, and the light of the four faces turned above the market through every dark hour, steady, the way her father had made it, the way she had learned at last to keep it.
