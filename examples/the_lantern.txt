The Lantern

Mara kept the lighthouse the way her father had kept it, and his father before him. Every evening she climbed the ninety-seven steps, trimmed the wick, and set the great lamp burning. The fishing boats knew her light. The town below slept easy because it turned, steady and warm, through every dark hour. She loved the work. She loved the quiet. She wanted nothing more than for the light to go on turning after she was gone.

Then the storm came, the worst in forty years. The wind tore the shutters from their hinges and drove the rain sideways through the broken glass. In the morning the lamp was dark. The great lens had cracked from base to crown, and no glazier within a hundred miles could cut another. Mara stood in the cold gallery and felt the whole weight of it settle on her shoulders. Without the light, the reef was a grave waiting for the next boat.

Word came that the harbor council had voted. They would not pay to repair the tower. A new beacon, electric and automatic, would be built on the far headland, and the old lighthouse would be let go dark for good. Mara walked the length of the empty gallery that night with no lamp to tend. She thought of her father's hands on the brass. She had failed him, and failed the boats, and there was nothing left in her but ash and the sound of the sea eating the rocks below.

But in the grey hour before dawn she remembered the crates in the cellar, the ones her grandfather had never opened. She went down with a candle and pried them apart. Inside, packed in straw, lay the spare lens — cut and polished a lifetime ago, waiting for exactly this. Her hands shook as she carried the pieces up, ninety-seven steps, one careful trip at a time. She worked through the whole of the next day, fitting and sealing, cutting her fingers on the old brass and not caring.

That evening the light turned again. Mara stood in the gallery and watched the beam sweep out across the water, steady and warm, and the boats in the harbor blew their horns one after another until the whole bay was singing. The council came the next week and saw the light and quietly forgot about the headland. Mara climbed the steps that night the way she always had, and the town below slept easy, and the light went on turning.
